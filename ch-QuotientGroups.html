    Proposition 14.0.1.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(N\) a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Then, the left cosets of \(N\) form a partition of \(G\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\bigcup_{g\in G}gN=G \end{equation*}
    and for \(g,h\in G\text{,}\) either \(gN=hN\) or \(gN\cap hN=\emptyset\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(N\) a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) As \(gN\subseteq G\) for every \(g\in G\text{,}\) then the containment \(\displaystyle\bigcup_{g\in G}gN\subseteq G\) holds. To see that we have equality, let \(x\in G\) and note that since \(e\in N\text{,}\) we have \(x=xe\in xN\) so that \(x\in \displaystyle\bigcup_{g\in G}gN\) as well. What remains then is to let \(g,h\in G\text{,}\) assume that \(gN\cap hN\neq\emptyset\) and show that \(gN=hN\text{.}\) So let \(x\in gN\) and write \(x=gn\) for some \(n\in N\text{.}\) Also, since \(gN\cap hN\neq\emptyset\text{,}\) we know there is some \(y\in gN\cap hN\text{.}\) Write \(y=ga=hb\) for some \(a,b\in N\text{.}\) Multiplying on the right by \(a^{-1}\) yields \(g=hba^{-1}\text{.}\) Thus, \(x=gn=hba^{-1}n\text{.}\) Since \(a,b\in N\text{,}\) we know \(ba^{-1}n\in N\) and so \(x=h(ba^{-1}n)\in hN\text{,}\) which shows that \(gN\subseteq hN\text{.}\) A similar argument shows that \(hN\subseteq gN\) so that \(gN=hN\) as desired.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Now that we know the left cosets of a normal subgroup form a partition of the group, we know there is an associated equivalence relation. Letâ€™s work out the details of that. Let \([x]\) denote the set of a partition which contains the element \(x\text{.}\) The equivalence relation defined from a partition is
    \begin{equation*} x\sim y\Longleftrightarrow [x]=[y]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using the notation we have for the cosets, this becomes
    \begin{align*} g\sim h \amp\Longleftrightarrow gN=hN\\ \amp\Longleftrightarrow g\in hN\\ \amp\Longleftrightarrow \text{ there exists an }n\in N\text{ such that }g=hn\\\ \amp\Longleftrightarrow \text{ there exists an }n\in N\text{ such that }h^{-1}g=n\\ \amp\Longleftrightarrow h^{-1}g\in N \end{align*}
    and so our equivalence relation boils down to being
    \begin{equation*} g\sim h\Longleftrightarrow h^{-1}g\in N \end{equation*}
    which, we might have guessed based on the equivalence relation for ideals.
