    Definition 15.0.1. Group Homomorophisms.

    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be groups, with operations \(\star_G\) and \(\star_H\) respectively. A function \(\varphi:G\to H\) is called a group homomorphism (or simply homomorphism when itâ€™s clear weâ€™re discussing groups) if for every \(g_1,g_2\in G\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(g_1\star_G g_2)=\varphi(g_1)\star_H\varphi(g_2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 15.0.2. A "Natural" Surjection.

    Let \(G=\Z\) and \(H=\Z/2\Z\text{.}\) The function \(\varphi:\Z\to\Z/2\Z\) given by \(n\mapsto [n]\) is a surjective group homomorphism. Indeed,
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(n+m)=[n+m]=[n]+[m]=\varphi(n)+\varphi(m)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Definition 15.0.6. Group Isomorphisms.

    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be groups. A function \(\varphi:G\to H\) is called a group isomorphism (or simply isomorphism when it is clear weâ€™re discussing groups) if \(\varphi\) is a bijective homomorphism. When such an isomorphism exists, we say that \(G\) and \(H\) are isomorphic and denote this by \(G\cong H\text{.}\)
